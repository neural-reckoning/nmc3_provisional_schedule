Computation noise promotes cognitive resilience to adverse conditions in humans and recurrent networks

Valentin Wyart

Charles Findling, École Normale Supérieure

Abstract

Random noise in information processing systems is widely seen as detrimental to function. But despite the large trial-to-trial variability of neural activity, humans show a remarkable adaptability to unexpected adverse events during goal-directed behavior. The origin of this cognitive ability, described as constitutive of general intelligence, is still disputed. In this talk, I will show that implementing computation noise in recurrent neural networks boosts their cognitive resilience to a variety of adverse events entirely unseen during training, in a way that strikingly resembles human cognition in similar conditions. In contrast to neural networks featuring exact computations, noisy networks and humans acquire behavioral hallmarks of Bayesian inference in a ‘zero-shot’ fashion – without prior experience with conditions that require these computations for maximizing rewards. These findings suggest that human intelligence may ride on computation noise to promote near-optimal behavior in adverse conditions without any engineered cognitive sophistication.
