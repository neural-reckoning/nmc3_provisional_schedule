The gradient of population receptive field stimulus-dependence in ventral visual cortex

Garikoitz Lerma-Usabiaga

Garikoitz Lerma-Usabiaga; Rosemary Le; Chen Gafni; Michal Ben-Shachar; Brian Wandell

Abstract

Regions of ventral occipito-temporal (VOT) visual cortex are more responsive to written words than other categories of visual stimuli. We refer to these regions as the VOT reading circuitry (VOTRC). The population receptive field (pRF) of a voxel is the portion of the visual field in which stimuli evoke a response. The pRFs of voxels in the VOTRC are stimulus-dependent (Le et al., 2017). Measured with word stimuli, the pRF centers are mainly confined to the central visual field (with a clear contralateral bias); measured with checkerboard stimuli, the pRF centers span a wider range of locations, extending to the periphery. To understand this stimulus dependence, we analyzed pRFs in visual field-maps (V1-3, hV4, VO1) whose signals are the likely inputs to the VOTRC. We measured pRFs in 34 subjects (20 only English; 14 Hebrew/English) using the moving bar paradigm with several types of contrast patterns within the bar (checkerboards, English and Hebrew words of different sizes, false fonts). We found that stimulus dependence emerges gradually along the visual field map hierarchy. The stimulus dependence is small in visual areas V1 and V2, becoming more pronounced in V3, hV4, and VO-1. The pRF center can be expressed in Cartesian [x,y] or Polar [eccentricity,angle] coordinates. The largest variation in stimulus dependence is the eccentricity of the pRF center; the angle remains relatively constant. Given that the pRF position is stimulus dependent, it may be important to use the relevant stimulus when using pRFs to model the visual components of different tasks. With an interest in reading development, it seems best to use word stimuli in a legible orthography. Establishing pRF properties using such stimuli in good readers may help understand the visual reading circuitry and provide a baseline for comparing responses in poor readers.
