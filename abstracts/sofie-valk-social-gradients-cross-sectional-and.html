Social gradients: Cross-sectional and longitudinal evidence of differential functional embedding and plasticity of the social brain

Sofie Valk

Philipp Kanske, TU Dresden, Germany; Bo-yong Park, MNI, Canada; Seok-Jun Hong, Sungkyunkwan University, South Korea; Anne BÃ¶ckler-Raettig, University of Hannover, Germany; Fynn-Mathis Trautwein, University of Freiburg, Germany; Boris C. Bernhardt*, MNI, Canada; Tania Singer*, Social neuroscience lab, Germany

Abstract

Social interaction is key to human survival and has been proposed to be linked to the organization of the cerebral cortex. In a sample of healthy adults (N=332) we determined that task-based networks implicated in attention, empathy, and ToM-processing are topologically ordered along a social gradient from internal to external cognitive functions and have differentiable behavioral, functional, and genetic associations. We found domain-specific change in functional topology following a mental training intervention addressing attention and interoception, socio-affective skills, and socio-cognitive skills respectively. Attention-based practice resulted in segregation of cortical association networks, whereas socio-cognitive training resulted in their integration, reflecting a dissociation between cognitive quiescence and active thought. Socio-affective training had balancing effects on functional network organization, related to the regulating function of emotion. Together, our results show that social interaction involves a modifiable balance of network integration and segregation embedded along a social gradient from external to internal cognitive functions.
