Structures and functions of sleep circuits in the brain

Antoine Adamantidis

Carolina Gutierrez Herrera; Mattia Aime; Markus Schmidt

Abstract

Sleep is an essential biological need. Yet, its origin and functions are subject of intense experimental research (and debates). is My research interests aim at investigating the wiring, firing dynamics and plasticity of the neural circuits regulating brain states in normal and pathological states using in vitro and in vivo optogenetics combined to genetics and electophysiological, an now cell network imaging methods. These research goals have been driven by questions such as: What define a sleep/wake circuit? What are sleep oscillations good for? What is the neural coding of sleep and brain plasticity during sleep in health and diseases (narcolepsy, insomnia, stroke)?
My laboratory has identified hypothalamic cells controlling rapid-eye movement (REM) sleep and arousal, a septo-hippocampus circuit supporting theta rhythm generation and contextual memory consolidation during REM sleep and a thalamus sleep-wake switch. My current projects focuses on the orchestration of sleep oscillations (slow waves, spindles, theta, etc.) in space and time and their roles in brain plasticity after stroke, sleep-dependent memory consolidation, and maintenance of hypothalamic-based feeding (innate) behavior.
