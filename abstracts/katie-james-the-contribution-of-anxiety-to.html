The contribution of anxiety to spatial memory deficits in the TgF344-AD rat model of Alzheimer’s disease

Katie James

Laura E. Berkowitz, University of New Mexico; Ryan E. Harvey, University of New Mexico; Shannon M. Thompson, University of New Mexico; Carlos R. Olguin, University of New Mexico; Emma N. Drake, University of New Mexico; Nathan S. Pentkowski, University of New Mexico; Benjamin J. Clark, University of New Mexico

Abstract

Anxiety and spatial navigation impairment are both early markers of Alzheimer’s disease. The TgF344-AD rat model of Alzheimer’s disease has previously shown increased anxiety-like behaviors at 4 months, and spatial impairments at 7 to 10 months of age. The connection between anxiety and working memory is important to consider as previous research has highlighted the negative association between anxiety and performance on working memory tasks. In light of this association, we sought to determine whether elevated levels of anxiety-like behaviors in TgF344-AD rats may be an early predictor of performance in a spatial working memory task. TgF344-AD rats (n = 16) and F344 controls (n = 12) were assessed across three time-points (4–5, 7–8, 10–11 months). The match-to-place water task and the elevated plus maze were used to evaluate spatial working memory and anxiety-like behaviors, respectively. We hypothesize that the magnitude of anxiety will be a large predictor of spatial working memory performance in TgF344-AD rats. This research will help inform future studies that examine contributing factors of spatial navigation impairment in AD, and may be useful for the development of targeted treatments.
