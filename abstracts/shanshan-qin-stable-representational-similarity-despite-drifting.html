Stable representational similarity despite drifting population activity in a network model of noisy representation learning

Shanshan Qin

Shanshan Qin, Harvard University; Cengiz Pehlevan, Harvard University

Abstract

Stable neural representations are generally associated with robust behavior and physiological functions. However, recent experiments have shown that neural population codes in many brain areas continuously change even when the animals have learned and stably perform their tasks. This representational ``drift'' naturally leads to questions about its causes, dynamics and functions. To address these questions, we consider a basic and biologically-plausible model of representation learning, the Hebbian/anti-Hebbian network, and hypothesize that drifting representation is due to noisy synaptic updates and turnover. We find that while the receptive fields of individual neurons change significantly over time, representational similarity of population codes is stable over time, providing a substrate for robust downstream readout by a Hebbian neuron. We investigate the dynamics of the drift, and find that the drift speed can be quantified by a rotational diffusion constant, which is determined by the learning rate, noise level and upstream input statistics. Our model accounts for many of the recent observed experimental observations in hippocampus and cortices related to sensorimotor tasks.
