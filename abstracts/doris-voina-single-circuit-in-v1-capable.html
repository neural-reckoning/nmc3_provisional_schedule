Single circuit in V1 capable of switching contexts during movement usingVIP population as a switch

Doris Voina

Stefano Recanatesi; Brian Hu; Eric Shea-Brown; Stefan Mihalas

Abstract

As animals adapt to their environments, their brains are tasked with processing stimuli in different sensory contexts. This suggests that neural architectures have evolved to respond flexibly to a range of stimulus conditions and statistics. As the number of possible contexts is virtually infinite, making parallel circuits for each context unfeasible, a crucial question is what architectures permit implementation of multiple related computations within a single circuit. Here, we address this question in the specific case of the circuitry of the visual system, focusing on the role of distinct cell types in context integration of visual scenes. We show that a population of inhibitory neurons, the VIP cells, can enable such context integration — defined as the integration of feedforward and surround information across visual space — in two different contexts, in which the viewer is static vs. moving. Moreover, the VIP population is efficient, requiring only a relatively small number of neurons to switch contexts. Our findings shed light on a minimally complex architecture that is capable of switching between two naturalistic contexts using few switching units.
