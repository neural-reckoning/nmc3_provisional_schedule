Selective impacts of sleep deprivation on brain and behavior

marco bigica (mr)

Chunxiang Jiang, Cardiff University Brain Research Imaging Centre, Cardiff University, Cardiff, UK and Paul C. Lauterbur Research Center for Biomedical Imaging, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Shenzhen, China; Ilenia D'Onofrio, Cardiff University Brain Research Imaging Centre, Cardiff University, Cardiff, UK; Zhishan Liu, Cardiff University Brain Research Imaging Centre, Cardiff University, Cardiff, UK; Chen Song, Cardiff University Brain Research Imaging Centre, Cardiff University, Cardiff, UK and Department of Psychiatry, University of Wisconsin-Madison, Madison, USA

Abstract

Sleep is essential to brain functioning and lack of sleep greatly impairs behavior, yet the mechanisms underlying the detrimental effects of sleep deprivation remain largely unclear. Here we investigate how sleep deprivation influences the structure of neural connections and the function of neural communications that, in turn, lead to the behavioral impairments.

Using a combination of quantitative structural MRI, multi-compartment diffusion MRI, resting-state functional MRI, task functional MRI, behavioral testing and physiological monitoring, we traced the changes in brain structural indices (axon density, axon diameter, myelination), functional indices (signal propagation speed, signal-to-noise ratio, coding accuracy), behavioral indices (perception, decision making, introspection, attention, vigilance, action, emotion) and physiological indices (pupillary light reflex, resting heart rate, resting breathing pattern), across 36 hours of sleep deprivation and one night of recovery sleep, in 14 healthy young adults.

Analysis of behavioral data revealed that the time course of performance detriment is largely dissociable between behavioral domains. The detriment was first observed in vigilance and mood ratings, followed by perceptual decision making and introspection. By contrast, visual spatial attention and perception remain relatively unaffected by sleep deprivation. The heterogeneous changes in behavioral performances hint towards a stronger impact of sleep deprivation on prefrontal cortex than on parietal-occipital cortex, which we are currently testing via analysis of brain structural and functional data.
