TASER (Temporally Adaptive SourcE Reconstruction)

Ryan Timms

Ryan C. Timms, University of Oxford; Andrew J. Quinn, University of Oxford; Alexander Skates, University of Oxford; Cameron J. Higgins, University of Oxford; Usama Pervaiz, University of Oxford; Evan J. Roberts, University of Oxford; James Bonaiuto, Institut des Sciences Cognitives Marc Jeannerod; Stephen M. Smith, University of Oxford; Matthew J. Brookes, University of Nottingham; Gareth R. Barnes, University College London; and Mark W. Woolrich, University of Oxford

Abstract

Source reconstruction is a ubiquitous yet complex and critical pre-processing step in MEG and EEG analysis. Existing approaches have enjoyed great success, but nearly always assume stationarity in the source space covariance, a constraint which confounds the accuracy of source localisation (Barratt et al., 2018). Here we present TASER (Temporally Adaptive SourcE Reconstruction), a fully unsupervised algorithm which learns time-point specific reconstruction weights to build source space images. Specifically, we infer regularised time-varying data and noise covariance matrices at the same temporal resolution of the data. This is achieved by employing a stochastic gradient variational Bayesian inference framework, featuring gated recurrent unit neural networks. We show in simulation that TASER improves upon conventional static (minimum norm and beamformer) estimates in 3 out of 4 criteria. Applying the algorithm to real data, we show that TASER is able to uncover task-relevant physiological dynamics in an unsupervised fashion, negating the need to manually segregate data into different trial conditions. By automatically accounting for differences in covariance between timepoints, we replicate the findings of Barratt et al. (2018) and map the post movement beta rebound of digits 1 and 5 in motor cortex using MEG data, demonstrating the increase in spatial resolution over conventional static approaches to source reconstruction. This new unsupervised technique has exciting potential applications to datasets where there is little to no pre-supposed task structure e.g. during the resting state.
