Neural and genetic bases for human ability traits

Camila Pinto

Jannis Bielefeld, Department of Physiology, Northwestern University Feinberg School of Medicine; Rami Jabakhanji, Department of Physiology, Northwestern University Feinberg School of Medicine;Diane Reckziegel,Department of Physiology, Northwestern University Feinberg School of Medicine; James W Griffith, Department of Medical Social Sciences, Feinberg School of Medicine, Northwestern University; Apkar Vania Apkarian, Department of Physiology, Northwestern University Feinberg School of Medicine, & Center for Translational Pain Research, Feinberg School of Medicine, Northwestern & Department of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation, Feinberg School of Medicine, Northwestern University & Department of Anesthesiology, Feinberg School of Medicine, Northwestern University.

Abstract

The understanding of human ability, including behavioral and neurological function, is still underexplored. Current attempts are often narrowly defined and lack a comprehensive basis. However, a better definition of a basis for human ability would be invaluable since neuropsychiatric disease diagnoses and symptom severity are commonly related to such differences in performance. Here, we use the framework of the National Institutes of Health (NIH) Toolbox, which provides a unified assessment of neurologic and behavioral function to identify four underlying ability traits within the NIH Toolbox normative data (n = 1369): 1: Motor-endurance, 2: Emotional processing, 3: Executive and cognitive function, and 4: Social interaction. Using the Human Connectome Project young adult dataset (n = 778) we showed that two traits, Motor-endurance and Executive and cognitive function were reliably associated with specific brain functional networks (r^2=0.305Â±0.021). Moreover, we showed the biological nature of these ability traits by calculating their heritability (31% and 49% respectively) from twin data.
Therefore, we characterize human ability using the NIH Toolbox as a framework. Looking forward, this framework allows us to determine connections between the trait space and clinical outcomes in different neurophysiological conditions, such as chronic pain.
