Lesions of the dorsal striatum increase the sensitivity to effort

Stefania Sarno

Stefania Sarno, Institut de Neurobiologie de la Mediterranée (INMED); Maria-TeresaJurado-Parras, Institut de Neurobiologie de la Mediterranée (INMED); MostafaSafaie, Institut de Neurobiologie de la Mediterranée (INMED); Bastien Berret, Université Paris-Saclay; David Robbe, Institut de Neurobiologie de la Mediterranée (INMED).

Abstract

Animals are able to learn sophisticated strategies to exploit the available resources in their environment while minimizing the metabolic cost required in this interaction. From a mathematical standpoint, this observation suggests that animal behavior can be well described within the framework of optimal control whereby the trajectories followed by animals optimize a cost function. In this work, we compare simulations in the optimal control framework with behavioral data for rats challenged in a time estimation task. Subsequently, we study how lesions of the dorsal striatum (dS) affect the strategy developed by rats. We found that rats behavior comply with optimality principles and that kinematic alterations induced by dS lesion are compatible with an increased sensitivity to effort.
