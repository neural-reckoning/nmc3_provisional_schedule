Nonlinear stimulus representations in neural circuits with approximate excitatory-inhibitory balance

vicky zhu

Vicky Zhu, University of Notre Dame; Cody Baker, University of Notre Dame;Robert Rosenbaum, University of Notre Dame

Abstract

Balanced excitation and inhibition is widely observed in cortex. How does this balance shape neural computations and stimulus representations? This question is often studied using computational models of neuronal networks in a dynamically balanced state. But balanced network models predict a linear relationship between stimuli and population responses. So how do cortical circuits implement nonlinear representations and computations? We show that every balanced network architecture admits stimuli that break the balanced state and these breaks in balance push the network into a “semi-balanced state” characterized by excess inhibition to some neurons, but an absence of excess excitation. The semi-balanced state produces nonlinear stimulus representations and nonlinear computations, is unavoidable in networks driven by multiple stimuli, is consistent with cortical recordings, and has a direct mathematical relationship to artificial neural networks. In addition, we study the synaptic plasticity and learnings in semi-balanced networks, and aim to build a more direct analogue between ANNs and neuronal networks in the cerebral cortex. This will open a highway of shared information between neuroscience and machine learning, resulting in rapid advances in each field.
