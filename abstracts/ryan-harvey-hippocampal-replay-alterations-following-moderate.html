Hippocampal replay alterations following moderate prenatal alcohol exposure

Ryan Harvey

Ryan E. Harvey, University of New Mexico; Laura E. Berkowitz, University of New Mexico; Benjamin J. Clark, University of New Mexico

Abstract

Prenatal alcohol exposure (PAE) is among the most common developmental insults to the nervous system and is characterized by memory impairment. There is a need to identify physiological changes that help explain this memory impairment. Hippocampal sharp-wave ripples (SPW-Rs) and associated replay events are potential candidates as they are the electrophysiological signatures of memory reactivation and consolidation. Here, we tested the hypothesis that SPW-Rs and replay events are altered following PAE. To test this hypothesis, we recorded single-unit and local field potentials from hippocampal subregions CA1 & CA3 of moderate PAE & control rats while they made laps on a linear track and while at rest on a pedestal. Preliminary findings suggest that replay and SPW-Rs are altered following PAE. Specifically, replayed trajectories are truncated and SPW-R frequencies are slowed. The results from these experiments provide a novel framework for investigating hippocampal mechanisms of memory impairment associated with PAE and may also provide potential functional biomarkers following PAE.
