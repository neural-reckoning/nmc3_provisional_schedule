Body silhouettes as features in visual search: evidence from spatially-global attention modulation in visual cortex

Sushrut Thorat

Marius V. Peelen, Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behaviour, Radboud University, Nijmegen, The Netherlands.

Abstract

Visual search for objects in cluttered scenes is guided by top-down attention, allowing observers to efficiently select goal-relevant information from incoming visual input. When search is for low-level features, such as orientation, color, or motion direction, this attentional selection modulates visual processing across the visual field, even at spatially-unattended and task-irrelevant locations. Recent behavioral and neuroimaging studies have provided evidence that similar spatially-global selection mechanisms may exist for highly-familiar object categories such as faces. Similar to low-level features, faces are selectively represented in the visual system. In the present fMRI study, we reveal spatially-global modulation for another selectively-represented object category: human bodies. Participants were cued to search for one of six object categories in two vertically-aligned images. Crucially, two additional, horizontally-aligned, images were simultaneously presented but were never task-relevant across three experimental sessions. Analyses locked to the objects presented in these task-irrelevant images revealed that responses evoked by body silhouettes were strongly modulated by the participantsâ€™ top-down attentional set, becoming more body-selective when participants searched for bodies in the task-relevant images. These effects were observed both in univariate analyses of body-selective cortex and in multivariate analyses of high-level visual cortex. Finally, we found that this modulation reflected response gain rather than a generic bias induced by the cues. These findings provide evidence for a spatially-global selection mechanism for human bodies, linking this modulation to body-selective representations in high-level visual cortex.
