Is the Kiki-Bouba effect specific to pronounceable words?

Ananya Passi

SP Arun, Indian Institute of Science Bangalore

Abstract

INTRODUCTION. Across cultures, people associate spiky shapes to the word “Kiki” and rounded shapes to the word “Bouba”. What causes this highly systematic shape-sound association? One possibility is that our mouth forms an angular shape while uttering the word “Kiki” and circular shape while saying “Bouba”. Alternatively, objects with sharp edges produce high-pitched (“Kiki”-like) sounds while those with rounded edges produce low-pitched (“Bouba”-like) sounds. Repeated cross-modal experiences could enable learning this association. Here, we investigated the reason behind this effect using a series of behavioral experiments.

METHODS. We conducted four online behavioral experiments using Google Forms. Ten spiky and ten rounded shapes were created. In Experiments 1 & 2, subjects had to associate each shape to a Kiki-like or Bouba-like word. In Experiment 3 & 4, multiple audio clips were played and subjects had to associate them with either a spiky or a rounded shape. The audio clips were either (1) pronounceable Kiki & Bouba words; (2) the same words but reversed in time so that they were no longer pronouncable, but contained the same sound frequencies; (3) pure tones matched to the mean frequency of the Kiki and Bouba words; and (4) Sounds produced by natural objects when struck.

RESULTS. In the shape-validation experiment, 80% of the subjects associated spiky shapes with Kiki-like words and rounded shapes with Bouba-like words. In Experiments 3 & 4, subjects associated Bouba-like words with rounded shapes (~85%), Kiki-like words with spiky shapes(~60%), reversed Bouba words with rounded shapes (~85%), reversed Kiki words with spiky shapes (~55%), metal clanks with spiky shapes (~90%) and pillow hits with rounded shapes (~55%). There was a strong positive correlation between mean frequency of sound and the Kiki-ness of the word.

CONCLUSIONS. We conclude that the Kiki-Bouba effect reflects a robust association between shapes and sound frequency irrespective of whether the sounds can be pronounced.
