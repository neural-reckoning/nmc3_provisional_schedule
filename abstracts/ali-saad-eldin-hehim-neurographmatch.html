NeuroGraphMatch

Ali Saad-Eldin (he/him)

Benjamin D. Pedigo, Johns Hopkins University; Youngser Park, Johns Hopkins University; Carey E. Priebe, Johns Hopkins University; Joshua T. Vogelstein, Johns Hopkins University

Abstract

A network is defined as a set of nodes connected by a set of edges. In neuroscience, the structure of the brain (connectome) can be modeled as a network, where neurons or regions of interest are thought of as nodes, and the connections between these regions are thought of as edges. Through the use of functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) as well as diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), one can estimate the presence of “nodes” and “edges”in the brain, thus representing it as a graph. Additional advancements in methodologies involving electron microscopy have allowed for the ability to capture information on individual neurons and synapses. This has made the use of network statistics important in the study of brains. Specifically, the graph matching algorithm (also known as the quadratic assignment problem) seeks to quantify the underlying similarity between two networks, determining an alignment of nodes between them. This analysis can be utilized to quantify similarities in inter- and intra-species connectomes, or to predict homologous neuron pairs across brain hemispheres. Through the use of available and simulated data, we will demonstrate the efficacy of our Fast Approximate Quadratic assignment algorithm (FAQ) Python implementation (now in SciPy and graspologic), and showcase it’s applications in the fields of computational neuroscience and, more broadly, combinatorial optimization.
