The spatial extent of the tilt aftereffect

Busra Tugce Gurbuz

Huseyin Boyaci, Bilkent University, National Magnetic Resonance Research Center (UMRAM)

Abstract

The tilt aftereffect (TAE) is observed when the perceived tilt of oriented contour changes due to adaptation to another orientated contour. So far, the TAE has generally assumed as a local aftereffect observed only at the adapted location. Here, we challenged this main assumption and investigated how the TAE spreads across different spatial locations.
Using a peripheral adapter, we measured the TAE magnitude at 15 peripheral locations while participants were fixating on a central fixation point. Adapter grating was always presented at the same spatial location with 10.5° eccentricity to the central fixation point. In contrast, test gratings were presented randomly at 15 test locations within the same visual hemifield, including the adapter location, and two 1-up-1-down staircases determined their orientations. Participants indicated the test gratings' perceived tilt by pressing the right or left arrow key on a keyboard. The control condition was identical to the experimental condition except for adaptation. The subjective verticality (SV) of the test grating was determined by fitting a logistic regression function to the data. The TAE magnitude was calculated by subtracting the control condition's SV from the experimental condition's SV.
We found that the TAE is a non-local aftereffect that spreads across the whole visual hemifield (N=19, p<0.05) except one location (down-row, 2.5° eccentricity, p=0.472). To explain the computational mechanisms underlying this spread, we simulated a population of orientation-tuned neurons with different receptive fields (RFs). Based on the gain-reduction model of adaptation, we hypothesized that adaptation would decrease tuning-curves' and RFs’ sensitivity and trained our model with human data to explore how this reduction happens. Our trained-model could predict human data with 70% accuracy and provide supporting evidence for the gain-reduction model.
