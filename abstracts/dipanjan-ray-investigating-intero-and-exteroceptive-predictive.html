Investigating Intero- and Exteroceptive Predictive Processes in Major Depressive Disorder

Dipanjan Ray

Moumita Das, Basque Center for Applied Mathematics

Abstract

The association between depression and derangement of sensory processing is well known. However, our understanding of the neural processes underlying sensory perception, both intero- and exteroception, is undergoing a rapid change. Predictive coding, for example, is a theoretical framework that proposes that the brain constantly generates hypotheses that predict sensory input at varying levels of abstraction and minimizes prediction errors either by updating its prior hypotheses (perceptual inference) or by modifying sensory input through action (active inference). In other words, (i) both bottom up and top down information flow among hierarchical cortical regions play important role in sensory processing and (ii) sensory and motor processes are highly entangled. In the current study, we investigate whether and how depression alters top-down and bottom-up information flow in the sensory (and motor) cortex. We implement effective connectivity analysis among the brain regions involved in intero- and exteroception as well as motor processing employing Dynamic Causal Modelling on resting state fMRI data from 62 individuals diagnosed with major depressive disorder (MDD) and 62 age and sex matched healthy controls. We find alteration in effective connectivity in MDD patients in areas involved in exteroception and motor processing (e.g., V1→FP1, MC→V1, VLPFC→SMA, SMA→V1) but not in areas involved in interoception. The implications of these findings for the pathophysiology of depression is discussed.
