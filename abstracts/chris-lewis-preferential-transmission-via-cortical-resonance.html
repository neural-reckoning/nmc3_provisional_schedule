Neuromatch 3 / / Track 8 / Traditional talk

Preferential transmission via cortical resonance

Chris Lewis

Chris Lewis, University of Zurich; Jianguang Ni, Ernst Strüngmann Institute; Thomas Wunderle, Ernst Strüngmann Institute; Patrick Jendritza, Ernst Strüngmann Institute; Andreea Lazar, Ernst Strüngmann Institute; Ilka Diester, Ernst Strüngmann Institute; Pascal Fries, Ernst Strüngmann Institute

Abstract

Synchronization has been implicated in neuronal communication, but causal evidence remains indirect. We used optogenetics to emulate synaptic inputs to cat visual cortex and measured spike output. Channelrhodopsin 2 generated currents through the somato-dendritic membranes of excitatory neurons under precise temporal control. Constant excitation was transformed into strong synchronization in the gamma-band, revealing the well-known cortical resonance. Ramp stimulation demonstrated that the resonant frequency varied (30-60 Hz) with excitatory input strength, and Gaussian excitation exhibited hysteresis of power and frequency. Crucially, white-noise sequences enabled a causal analysis of network transmission, establishing preferential transmission of input components that are coherent to the resonance. Models composed of recurrently coupled excitatory and inhibitory units uncovered a crucial role of feedback inhibition and suggest that hysteresis can arise from the non-inactivating M-current. The presented approach provides a means to investigate resonance properties of local circuits and how these properties transform input and shape transmission.
