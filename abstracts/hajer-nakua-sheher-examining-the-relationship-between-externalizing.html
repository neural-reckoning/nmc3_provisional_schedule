Examining the relationship between externalizing and internalizing behaviour and the cortico-amygdalar network in children with neurodevelopmental disorders: A multi-modal dimensional study

Hajer Nakua (she/her)

Hajer Nakua, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Colin Hawco, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Natalie Forde, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Grace Jacobs, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Michael Joseph, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Aristotle Voineskos, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Anne L. Wheeler, SickKids Hospital, University of Toronto; Meng-Chuan Lai, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Peter Stazmari, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto; Beth Kelley, Queens University; Xudong Liu, Queens University; Stelios Georgiades, McMaster University; Rob Nicolson, University of Western Ontario; Jennifer Crosbie, SickKids Hospital, University of Toronto; Russell Schachar, SickKids Hospital, University of Toronto; Evdokia Anagnostou, Holland Bloorview Kids Rehabilitation Hospital; Jason Lerch, SickKids Hospital, University of Toronto; Paul Arnold, Cumming School of Medicine, University of Calgary; Stephanie Ameis, Centre of Addiction and Mental Health, University of Toronto

Abstract

Purpose: Children with neurodevelopmental disorders (NDDs) including autism spectrum disorder (ASD), obsessive compulsive disorders (OCD) and attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) exhibit high rates of externalizing and internalizing behaviours. These behaviours have been linked to variation in cortico-amygdalar circuitry in typically developing children (TDC). Here, we examine whether cortico-amygdalar variation relates to internalizing and externalizing behaviours in children with different NDDs.
Methods: Using data from the Province of Ontario Neurodevelopmental Disorder (POND) Network, we analyzed T1 weighted, resting state fMRI and diffusion weighted data from a transdiagnostic sample of children and youth (ages 6-18 years, n=157-346) with a primary diagnosis of ASD, ADHD, or OCD, and TDC. Cross-sectional behavioral assessments from the parent-report Child Behavioral Checklist (CBCL) and multi-modal imaging were used. Associations between externalizing or internalizing behavior and cortico-amygdalar network connectivity indices (i.e. structural covariance, functional connectivity white matter connectivity) were examined using linear regressions, controlling for age, gender, and modality specific covariates.
Results: No significant associations (or diagnostic group-by-behavior interaction effects) were found between externalizing or internalizing behavior and any of the connectivity indices examined.
Conclusions: The null results of the current study do not provide evidence of a shared relationship between cortico-amygdalar network connectivity properties and externalizing or internalizing behavior across a transdiagnostic sample of children and youth. Using larger sample sizes, comprehensive behavioral batteries, and shifting the theoretical framework for examining brain-behavior relationships in transdiagnostic samples may be necessary for delineating such relationships.
